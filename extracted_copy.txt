Services.
Based in the West Kootenay region of British Columbia, Climatic Resources Consulting (CRC) is a data-driven, climate resilience software and consulting company.  We provide leading edge climate projections and quantitative risk analysis that facilitate the
 of climate risks within asset management structures.  The approach and related software (
 Climate Analysis and Projections or LCap) uses statistically downscaled model projection data to provide the probability of threshold exceedance for a variety of climate indices that are critical for infrastructure and/or operations. 
Localised Climate Data
The recent increase in availability of on-line climate change information is a positive development that should facilitate the incorporation of climate projections into the planning process.  However, caution should be exercised when using the data.  For example, in areas of significant topography such as British Columbia, the temperature information will reflect the minimum grid size which will often include some proportion of higher, cooler elevations.  Consequently, modelled historical and projected temperature-derived indices may not reflect warmer valley-bottom conditions where most communities are situated. 
Observed precipitation, and in particular extreme precipitation indices, tend to be highly variable with respect to location.  At some, but not all, locations the observed maximum 1-day rainfall may exceed
 historical and even projected values.  Planners should ensure that the observed maximum 1-day precipitation do not exceed projected values before applying these numbers in the planning process.  
In order to derive accurate estimates of the probability of exceeding critical temperature and precipitation index thresholds from the model data, steps are taken to 1) ensure the model temperature data is adjusted to the baseline conditions at the location of interest and 2) the observed extreme

LCap Demo
In collaboration with the City of Edmonton, Climatic Resources Consulting (CRC) has developed a software package (Localized Climate Analysis and Projection – Lcap) that enables planners and engineers to use a probability-based approach to undertake targeted climate change planning for specific community operations and infrastructure. 
The software package uses the Pacific Climate Impacts Consortium’s ensemble of downscaled GCMs with 10 km X 10 km spatial resolution for locations Canada-wide and currently calculates 27 climate indices that are relevant for assessing extreme climate conditions.  Lcap is designed to work within an iterative process whereby community planners and engineers identify critical climate thresholds that apply to a range of specific operations and infrastructure. Lcap then enables the planning team to quantitatively assess the likelihood of when these thresholds will be exceeded and to make informed decisions on acceptable levels of risk and appropriate adaptation measures.  Further, the process facilitates the exploration of multiple probable climate futures for communities.

NORDIC SKI AREA CASE STUDY
CRC undertook this study to provide quantitative information that supported the planning process for a Nordic ski operation in British Columbia.  The report documents the climate history of the study area over the last 100 years and projections for future climate conditions over the 21st century.  In addition, CRC’s LCap software provided the probabilities of exceedance for a key climate threshold that is critical for ski operations. This data informed decisions around the allocation of resources at low vs high elevations sites and the development of strategies that will minimize the consequences of the anticipated impacts.
In terms of mean annual temperature, the climate in the study region has warmed at a rate of +1.6oC over the last 100 years.  Over the same period, the observed rate of warming globally has been approximately 1 oC, and it is anticipated that the local rate of warming will continue to exceed the global rate over the course of this century.  The rate of warming in the area has increased to +2.8oC /century over the last 50 years. In the winter months (DJF), the rate of warming over the last 50 years has been 3.3oC /century.  The rates of change in annual and seasonal precipitation when considered over the last 50 years are less striking.  
Model projections indicate that additional warming can be expected in the study area over the coming decades, and that this warming will be significant (Figure 1).  Increases in mean annual temperature are expected to reach between +2.8 oC (Low Carbon pathway - RCP4.5) and +3.5 oC (High Carbon pathway - RCP8.5) above the 1961-1990 base period by the 2050s.  The projected warming during winter (DJF) will be similar in magnitude to the annual warming throughout the century with winter temperatures exceeding the base period by between +2.7 oC (Low Carbon pathway) and +3.5 oC (High Carbon pathway) by the 2050s. 
Exploration of the relationship between climate and ski conditions revealed that Mean January/February/March Maximum Temperature (winter Tmax) has an important influence on ski conditions. Both qualitative assessments of ski conditions (derived from trail grooming logs) and the number of days after Jan 1 with > 5cm snow depth are both correlated with winter Tmax. A threshold of Winter Tmax greater than 4oC was identified as key for ski operations and is associated with unfavourable ski/skate conditions.  In addition, favourable ski conditions, such as those experienced in the 2018 and 2019 seasons, are associated with Winter Tmax of about -0.5 oC (Figure 2).  Probability plots (Figures 3a – 3b, 4a – 4b) provide the likelihood of winter Tmax exceeding the critical thresholds associated with unfavourable (and favourable) ski conditions.  
By the 2050s 1 in 4 years will qualify as a poor year in terms of snow conditions on the existing ski club trails.  In these years, ski seasons would be very short or even non-existent.  Further, although there have been some recent years with favourable ski conditions, they are not likely to be common in the future.  By 2050, there is a less than 7% chance of favourable conditions similar to 2018 and 2019 occurring on any given year. 
The recommended approach for using the above information involves establishing the Nordic club’s risk appetite for unfavourable ski conditions and then determining from the probability plots if the risk appetite will be exceeded within the planning timeframe.  For example, if 2050 is identified as the planning target, the likelihood of poor ski conditions associated with exceeding mean Winter Tmax of 4oC in any given year is approximately 24% (Figure 3b).  If the club determined that the acceptable risk appetite this threshold is 20% (or 1 year in 5), then it would be reasonable to conclude that any resource allocation for these operations beyond the 2050s would be inappropriate.  Further, by the 2050s, the likelihood of favourable ski conditions associated with Winter Tmax of -0.5 oC are about 7% (Figure 4b) which may reinforce this conclusion.  Following this procedure, exploration of different risk appetites and planning timeframes should constrain the decision-making process to scenarios in which resources are allocated within the range of risk appetite for unfavourable ski conditions at the ski area.

Probability of Exceeding a Budgetary Threshold for Building Cooling Costs.
For the purpose of demonstration, CRC has developed a hypothetical example of how LCap can be used to assess risks associated with exceeding budgetary thresholds. The data is fictitious but loosely based on cooling demand numbers for facilities in Edmonton AB.
In this case, the community is concerned about rising cost for cooling buildings in the summer. Since 1900, Cooling Degree Days (CDD) have been rising (Figure 1) as have the costs associated with cooling buildings. The first step is to establish the relationship between the energy used in cooling the facilities and Cooling Degree Days (Figure 2). The community identified an unacceptable annual cooling cost for the buildings of $143,000.00 which, based on the observed correlation between annual MWh and CDD (Figure 2), would result from an annual use of 1300MWh. Although the observed maximum annual energy used in cooling the buildings has not exceeded 930MWh, the community was interested in quantifying the likelihood of exceeding the 1300MWh threshold in the future.
Downscaled model projection for the community show that annual CCD will rise over the coming decades (Figure 3a) and that 1300 CDD per annum will become more common in the future. Quantitative estimates of the probability of exceeding 300 CDD (Figure 3b), provide financial planners with a means of assessing when action will need to be taken to avoid exceeding the budgetary threshold of $143,000.00 in annual cooling costs. The analysis shows that, by ca. 2050, there will be a 20% chance of exceeding the 300 CDD threshold with current cooling infrastructure configuration and efficiency. The analysis also demonstrates that, by ca. 2068, there will be a 50% chance of this threshold being exceeded each year. If financial planners are uncomfortable with a 1 in 5 chance of exceeding the budget, then action should be taken before ca. 2050 to reduce cooling costs.
